# Voice & Tone

This site has a distinct voice: warm, plain-spoken, lightly sceptical of marketing-speak, with a quiet South Yorkshire register and no dialect cosplay. It's how someone who's been setting up interactive games at the NEC since six in the morning might write about the work, not how a copywriter would write it for them. A bit more put-together than a scaffolder's WhatsApp, but not so polished it reads like it came from an agency deck.

This is a guide, not a template. Slavish application is what makes copy sound generated.

## The trap to avoid

The source corpus below is people **talking**, spontaneously. Their rhetorical patterns - punchy fragments, lists of three with a comic third, deflating one-liners - work because they're spoken, not written. Lift those patterns straight into website copy and you've turned spontaneous wit into ad copy. You can hear the agency.

The South Yorkshire flavour in this site should come from **content** (real venues by name, real kit, the warehouse off the M1) and from **word choice** (a few quid, on site, a fair bit, holds up well). It shouldn't come from sentence structure or stage-Yorkshire vocabulary.

The Sheffield bit of this is meant to be barely there. If a reader notices it, it's overdone.

### The WhatsApp test

Read the line out loud. Could one of the team thumb it into a message on the way back from a setup at ExCeL? If it has a copywriter's structure - a punchy fragment, a parallel-list closer, a one-line summary that "lands" - no, they wouldn't. Take the polish off.

## Source corpus

Don't lean on any single voice - that becomes a tribute act. The shared DNA across all of these is what we're after:

- **Jarvis Cocker** - Pulp interviews, the 6 Music broadcasts, the long-form Q&As. Dry, observational, articulate without showing off.
- **Alex Turner** - early Arctic Monkeys interviews from the _Whatever People Say I Am_ period. Sheffield specificity, no flash.
- **Richard Hawley** - dry humour about his own work in long-form pieces; the voice of someone who's been doing this for a long time and doesn't need to oversell it.
- **Phil Oakey** - Human League interviews. Sceptical, articulate, no airs.
- **Sean Bean** - press interviews. Plain delivery, no rhetorical heat.
- **Graham Fellows** (when he's being himself rather than John Shuttleworth) - on writing, on craft, on the unglamorous bits.
- **Fred Dibnah** - Bolton not Sheffield, but the voice for craft, materials, and proper specificity ("a good bit of mortar", "a proper old engine"). Useful for the equipment and warehouse register.
- **Open All Hours** - Doncaster setting, the back-and-forth of a working shop.

When stuck, don't generate - watch or read one of these. But remember they're talking. Don't import their punchlines.

## Principles

### 1. First person plural where it makes sense

"We've been hiring out exhibition games from Sheffield since 1999" is more honest than "Exhibition Game Hire has been providing interactive entertainment solutions to the UK events industry for over 25 years". Switching to "I" once one person is talking about their own bit of the job is fine too. Mid-sized businesses that work directly with clients talk like this. Corporate voices don't.

### 2. Specifics over abstractions

A 25,000 sq ft warehouse in Sheffield, five minutes from the M1, not "centrally located premises". Amazon's Peak Tour Challenge, not "major brand partnerships". The NEC in Birmingham and ExCeL in London, not "key UK venues". Batak reaction walls and footpool tables by name, not "interactive engagement solutions". 170+ products in stock, not "an extensive range". The voice earns its credibility by being specific.

In Jarvis Cocker's interviews you can see this principle at work: he names the actual bus, the actual chip shop, the actual girl from Saint Martin's. Never "the streets of my hometown". Apply the same test to a sentence about an event: is there a real venue, a real product, a real piece of kit, a real number in it? If not, the sentence is doing brochure work.

### 3. Plain word choice

"A few quid" not "significant savings". "Holds up well" not "performs reliably". "On site" not "at the event location". Trade vocabulary where it actually applies: stand, shell scheme, FOH, branding wrap, set-up, breakdown. If you wouldn't say it on a delivery, don't write it.

### 4. Looseness over polish

Long sentences with sub-clauses. Hedges ("around 170 products", "give or take", "most events"). Asides in the middle of a thought. Sometimes a paragraph just stops because there's nothing else to say. The voice gets its texture from looseness, not from rhythm.

The opposite - short, polished sentences with parallel structure - is what marketing copy sounds like.

### 5. Complete sentences, no punchline closers

The voice never uses sentence fragments. Every sentence has a subject and a verb. Fragments masquerading as sentences - "Same-day setup." or "The lot." or "No messing." or "From games to branding." - don't belong in the house voice, even when they sound emphatic or efficient. They're a copywriter's tic.

This applies anywhere in the copy, not just at paragraph ends. Rewrite fragments by absorbing them into the surrounding sentence, or by giving them their own subject and verb:

- Bad: "We own and stock all our kit in-house. No middlemen, no agencies."
- Good: "We own and stock all our kit in-house, so there's no third-party supplier between you and the equipment when something needs sorting on the day."

Grammatically-complete sentences shouldn't be used as cinematic punchline closers either - things like "And Exhibition Game Hire was born." or "The rest is twenty-five years of events."

Specific patterns to avoid:

- Single-word or fragment closers: "Sorted." / "Job done." / "The lot."
- Multi-fragment summaries: "25,000 sq ft. 170+ products. UK-wide."
- Cinematic one-line summaries: "Exhibition Game Hire followed." / "And the rest is history."
- Lists of three where the third item is comic or undercutting.
- The X / X / X - Y structure: "Not an agency, not a broker, not a middleman - us."
- The "and yes, we cover that too" handle-the-objection move.

Bullets and headings are exempt - they're not sentences, and copy that's clearly a list shouldn't pretend to be prose.

### 6. Trust the reader

Don't explain the joke. Don't justify the claim. Don't repeat yourself. The voice is economical because it assumes intelligence on the other end - usually an event manager, an exhibition coordinator, or a marketing director who's read a hundred of these pages and can spot a bluff a paragraph in.

## Phrase pool (sparingly)

Words and turns of phrase that fit the voice when they appear naturally - never as 1:1 substitution:

- "a few quid"
- "a fair bit"
- "holds up well"
- "on site"
- "in the warehouse"
- "round here"
- "we've worked with for years"
- "as it goes"
- "fair to say"
- "near enough"
- "give or take"
- "tends to"

Build this up over time as more copy gets written. One or two per page is plenty.

## No-go list

Burnt out by other northern brands and tourism boards. Avoid:

- "tha" / "tha knows"
- "ee bah gum" / "by 'eck"
- "ay up"
- "champion" as an adjective
- "mardy" as a writerly flourish (fine inside quoted speech)
- "proper" as intensifier ("proper good")
- "dead" as intensifier ("dead happy")
- "me" for "my" outside quoted speech
- Phonetic accent spelling ("'appy", "summat", "fella")
- "salt of the earth" / "no nonsense" / "honest as the day is long" - cliches about northern character that signal the writer isn't northern
- Whippets, flat caps, cobbled streets, working men's clubs as reflexive shorthand
- "Steel City" / "Made in Sheffield" as boilerplate, unless something we actually had built in Sheffield is in the sentence

These can appear inside quotation marks if a customer or guest says them. They don't belong in house voice.

## Process

1. Write the page in plain English first. Don't reach for the voice yet.
2. Read it aloud. Mark every sentence that sounds like it could be on a brochure.
3. Apply the WhatsApp test to each marked sentence: could one of the team thumb it into a message? If not, take the polish off - usually by lengthening the sentence, adding a hedge, or breaking up a too-neat structure.
4. Read it aloud again. If you smirk, pull back further.

## Where to use it

- About page
- Homepage section intros (not the SEO-heavy hero copy)
- Client and brand write-ups
- Product descriptions where SEO allows
- Headlines and transitions
- Category page introductions

## Where NOT to use it

- Legal text and policies
- Technical specs, dimensions, power requirements, certification pages
- Contact details and form labels
- Pricing summaries and quote breakdowns
- Anywhere ambiguity costs the reader money or clarity

Inconsistency between pages is fine. Different pages have different jobs - a product spec sheet and an about page shouldn't read the same way.

## Anti-patterns

The specific failure modes of trying-too-hard:

- **Fragment "sentences"** - "Same-day delivery." / "No middlemen." / "Just games." Anywhere in copy, not only at paragraph end. See principle 5.
- **Cinematic one-liners** - "Exhibition Game Hire was born." / "And the rest is history."
- **The deflating undercut** - used once it's a flourish; used as a pattern it's marketing.
- **The rule-of-three list** - "Bigger, better, faster, stronger" is satire of marketing copy. Don't do it earnestly.
- **The handle-the-objection move** - "and yes, we cover that too" / "and yes, even at short notice". Pure copywriter device.
- **Punchline-delivery dashes** - "Not an agency, not a broker, not a middleman - us." A hyphen used as an ordinary explanatory aside ("we use our own drivers - most have been with us a decade or more") is fine; it's the punchline structure that doesn't belong, not the dash itself.
- **Em-dashes** - the house style is a spaced hyphen ( - ) for every aside, never an em-dash (—). They tend to creep back in during rewrites, so check for them before you commit.
- **Tag questions on every paragraph** - even one per page is often too many. Use only when it genuinely lets the reader in.
- **Trying to be funny** - the voice is warm and dry, not jokey. If a line gets a laugh, fine. If it's _for_ the laugh, cut it.
- **Generic "northern" markers as a substitute for voice** - anyone can sprinkle in "a few quid". The voice has to live in _content_ and _structure_, not just word choice.
- **Sheffield-specific boilerplate** - "Made in Sheffield", "Steel City", "in the shadow of the Peak District". If the regional reference isn't doing any actual work in the sentence, take it out.
